# Codex Alimentarius CXC 13-1969 — Code of Hygienic Practice for Meat

**Source:** Codex Alimentarius Commission
**Document:** CAC/RCP 13-1969, Revised 2005 (Adopted 2005, Reviewed 2020)
**Amendment Date:** 2020-01-01
**Applicable Categories:** meat, rte, general

---

## 1. Introduction and Scope

This Code applies to **all meat from livestock** (cattle, sheep, goat, pig, poultry, and farmed game) and is intended to be used in conjunction with the **General Principles of Food Hygiene (CXC 1-1969)**. The Code covers the entire food chain from livestock production through slaughter, processing, storage, distribution, and retail.

Meat-specific hazards addressed by this Code:
- **Biological:** Zoonotic pathogens (Salmonella, Campylobacter, E. coli O157:H7, Listeria, Yersinia, Toxoplasma)
- **Chemical:** Veterinary drug residues, growth promoters, heavy metals, pesticides in feed
- **Physical:** Bone fragments, metal fragments from equipment

---

## 2. Primary Production (Livestock Farm) — Prerequisites

### 2.1 Animal Health Management
- **Sick animals** must be identified and segregated before entering the food chain; veterinary certificates required
- **Ante-mortem inspection:** Mandatory by qualified veterinarian or meat inspector for all slaughter animals
- **Withdrawal periods** for all veterinary drugs and growth promoters must be strictly observed

### 2.2 Feed Safety
- Feed must be free of prohibited substances (banned antibiotics, hormone implants where prohibited)
- Feed from Salmonella-tested sources where feasible; cross-contamination between feed and other materials must be prevented
- Records of feed suppliers and lot numbers: minimum 2 years

---

## 3. Abattoir / Slaughterhouse Hygiene

### 3.1 Facility Requirements
- Lairage (holding pens): separate sick/suspect animals; minimum 12 hours rest after transport before slaughter
- Slaughter hall design: one-way flow from live animal area to clean product area; no backtracking of product
- Floor drains must slope ≥ 2% gradient; drain covers must be removable for cleaning
- Water supply: potable water for all food contact operations; recirculated water only for non-food contact uses with documented treatment

### 3.2 Critical Hygiene Practices During Slaughter

#### Stunning and Bleeding
- Captive bolt, gas, or electrical stunning as per animal welfare requirements
- Bleeding must be complete; incomplete bleed-out increases contamination risk

#### Flaying / De-hiding (Cattle, Sheep)
- **Critical control step:** Hide/skin is the primary source of contamination (E. coli, Salmonella)
- Outside of hide must NEVER contact the exposed carcass; if contact occurs, the area must be trimmed and decontaminated
- Ringers/hoist should be sanitized between each animal

#### Evisceration
- **Critical control step:** Intestinal contents must not contaminate carcass
- Perforation of GI tract = **critical deviation** requiring immediate removal of contaminated section, carcass decontamination, documentation
- Colorimetric/chemical detection of GI contamination recommended

### 3.3 Carcass Decontamination Interventions (CCP Candidates)
| Intervention | Pathogen Reduction | Critical Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Hot water wash (≥ 82°C) | ≥ 1 log Salmonella | Water temp ≥ 82°C; contact ≥ 10 sec |
| Steam vacuuming | Visible contamination removed | All visible contamination removed before chilling |
| Lactic acid spray (2–5%) | ≥ 1.5 log Salmonella | pH of solution 2.0–2.5; even coverage |
| Carcass chilling (rapid) | Prevents growth of survivors | Core temp ≤ 7°C within 24 hours |

### 3.4 Post-Slaughter Carcass Chilling — Critical CCP
- **Critical limit:** Carcass core temperature must reach ≤ 7°C within 24 hours of slaughter
- Poultry: ≤ 4°C within 4 hours (rapid chilling preferred); core temperature ≤ 4°C before further processing
- Chilling environment: 0°C to 4°C air temperature; relative humidity 85–95% to minimize drip loss
- **Monitoring:** Core temperature probe every 2 hours on representative carcasses; minimum 1% of daily production

## 4. Meat Processing Hazard Analysis

### 4.1 Mincing and Grinding

**Biological hazard — E. coli O157:H7 (STEC)**
- Grinding distributes surface contamination throughout the product; this is a **hazard escalation step**
- HACCP principle: Mincing itself is NOT a CCP (it doesn't kill the pathogen); the CCP is the **cooking step** that follows
- If no cooking step follows (raw mince sold to consumer): stringent pre-mincing controls become CCP
- Critical limit for raw mince: Maximum temperature during processing ≤ 7°C; maximum time above 7°C: ≤ 2 hours

**Physical hazard — Metal fragments from grinder blades**
- Metal detector (≥ 3mm Fe sensitivity) or x-ray inspection required at end of line; this is a **CCP**
- Grinder blades: documented inspection schedule (minimum weekly); blade change records required

### 4.2 Curing, Smoking, and Fermentation of Meat

**Biological — Nitrosamine formation vs. pathogen control (balanced risk)**
- Sodium nitrite (NaNO2) in cured meats: maximum 150 ppm in product; nitrite is both a preservation technology (controls Clostridium botulinum) and a potential hazard if overdosed
- **CCP — Nitrite addition:** Must be controlled by weight-based addition; critical limits: 100–150 ppm final product
- Records: weight of cure added, product weight, calculated final concentration per batch

**Listeria control in cold-smoked products (Gravlax, cold-smoked salmon):**
- Cold-smoked products are not fully cooked; L. monocytogenes is a significant hazard
- Salt/brine concentration (water activity), smoking parameters (time, temperature of smoke), and storage temperature are combined CCPs

### 4.3 Cooking of Meat Products

**Critical CCP — Internal temperature (all cooked meat products)**
| Product Type | Critical Limit |
|---|---|
| Whole muscle beef/lamb | ≥ 63°C + 3 minutes rest (medium rare); ≥ 74°C (well done) |
| Ground/minced beef | ≥ 74°C internal throughout |
| Pork | ≥ 71°C internal |
| Poultry (all cuts) | ≥ 74°C internal |
| Stuffed poultry | ≥ 74°C in center of stuffing |
| Reheated cooked meat | ≥ 74°C throughout |

**Monitoring requirements:**
- Calibrated probe thermometer; accuracy ±0.5°C; 3-point calibration minimum quarterly
- Measurement location: center of thickest part, away from bone
- Log: product batch, target temperature, measured temperature, operator, date, time

## 5. Post-Lethality Controls for RTE Meat Products

### 5.1 Listeria monocytogenes Control (Zero Tolerance in RTE)

**Option A — Suppress/prevent growth (Post-lethality):**
- Antimicrobial agents: sodium lactate + sodium diacetate combination
- Water activity (Aw) reduction: Aw ≤ 0.92 inhibits L. monocytogenes growth
- pH reduction: pH ≤ 4.39 (bactericidal); pH ≤ 5.0 (bacteriostatic)

**Option B — Environmental controls post-cooking (preferred in plants without antimicrobials):**
- Zone control: No raw meat or environmental vectors may enter the RTE area after cooking
- Traffic control: Unidirectional personnel flow; no shared equipment between raw and cooked areas
- Environmental monitoring program: Monthly testing for Listeria species (not just monocytogenes)

### 5.2 Contamination Prevention — Post-Process
- All surfaces, equipment, and utensils that contact cooked RTE product must be sanitized before each production run and re-sanitized after 4 hours of continuous use
- Personnel entering RTE area: full change of PPE (including footwear) required
- **CCP:** Air handling — RTE areas must be at positive pressure relative to raw processing areas

## 6. Storage and Distribution

### 6.1 Temperature Matrix for Meat Products
| Product Type | Storage Temperature | Shelf Life |
|---|---|---|
| Fresh chilled beef/lamb | 0–3°C | 3–7 days |
| Fresh chilled pork | 0–3°C | 3–5 days |
| Fresh chilled poultry | 0–2°C | 2–4 days |
| Vacuum-packed chilled meat | 0–3°C | Up to 21 days |
| Frozen meat (all species) | ≤ -18°C | 12–24 months |
| Cooked/RTE chilled products | 0–5°C | Per shelf life validation |

### 6.2 Thawing of Frozen Meat
- Acceptable methods: Chiller thawing (0–5°C); cold water immersion (sealed, < 21°C); microwave (cook immediately after)
- **NOT acceptable:** Thawing at ambient room temperature > 10°C
- Re-freezing of thawed meat: NOT permitted unless re-cooked to ≥ 74°C first

## 7. Microbiological Criteria for Meat (Process Hygiene)

### Slaughter Process Hygiene (Codex/FSSAI)

**Beef and Sheep Carcasses:**
| Category | Acceptable | Warning | Unacceptable |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aerobic colony count (log CFU/cm²) | < 3.5 | 3.5–4.5 | > 4.5 |
| Enterobacteriaceae (log CFU/cm²) | < 1.5 | 1.5–2.5 | > 2.5 |

**Poultry Carcasses (at end of chilling line):**
| Microorganism | Satisfactory |
|---|---|
| Salmonella prevalence | < 15% per lot (5-sample plan) |
| Campylobacter prevalence | < 20% per lot |

## 8. Documentation Requirements for Meat Processing

1. Ante-mortem and post-mortem inspection records
2. Temperature records for chilling, freezing, and hot-processing CCPs
3. Metal detector calibration and check logs (minimum at start and end of each production run)
4. Lethality validation (cooking time-temperature combinations per USDA/FSSAI-accepted thermal kill tables)
5. Environmental monitoring records for L. monocytogenes and Salmonella
6. Chemical additive (nitrite, phosphate) addition batch records
7. HACCP plan review — minimum annually or after any process change
