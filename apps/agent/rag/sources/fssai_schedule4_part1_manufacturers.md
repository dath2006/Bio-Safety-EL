# FSSAI Schedule 4 Regulations for Food and Dairy Manufacturers

## Scope and Legal Applicability
Under the Food Safety and Standards (Licensing and Registration of Food Businesses) Regulations, 2011, compliance with **Schedule 4** is a mandatory prerequisite for licensing.
- **Part II** establishes general sanitary and hygienic requirements for all food manufacturers, processors, and packagers.
- **Part III** establishes specific hygienic and sanitary practices for businesses engaged in the manufacture, processing, storing, and selling of **milk and milk products**.

Every Food Business Operator (FBO) must implement a Food Safety Management System (FSMS) plan based on HACCP and Good Manufacturing/Hygiene Practices (GMP/GHP) as outlined in these parts.

## 1. Temperature and Cooling Limits (Part III Specifics)
To stop the multiplication of pathogens, strict temperature ceilings are mandated throughout the supply chain:
- **Raw Milk Collection**: Must be cooled immediately to **4°C to 6°C or lower** upon daily procurement from producers and kept at that temperature until processing.
- **Post-Pasteurization Cooling**: Pasteurized milk must be immediately brought down to **4°C or lower**.
- **Storage Temperature**: The maximum storage temperature for pasteurized milk at the facility before dispatch cannot exceed **5°C**.
- **Refrigerated Dairy Products**: Any dairy product requiring refrigeration must be cooled as quickly as possible to its target durability temperature, with continuous automated recording of storage conditions.

## 2. Pasteurization Limits & Thermal Processing
Under FSSAI definitions, pasteurization is a validated microbicidal heat treatment designed to eliminate public health hazards without significantly altering quality:
- **High-Temperature Short-Time (HTST)**: Heated to at least **72°C (161°F) and held for a minimum of 15 seconds**, followed by rapid cooling. Flow diversion valves must automatically divert milk back for reprocessing if pasteurization temperature falls below 72°C.
- **Low-Temperature Long-Time (LTLT / Batch)**: Heated to at least **63°C (145°F) and held for a minimum of 30 minutes**.
- **Ultra-High Temperature (UHT)**: Thermal processing at **135°C to 150°C for 1 to 5 seconds** followed by aseptic packaging to secure shelf life at ambient storage.
- **Verification (Alkaline Phosphatase Test)**: Every pasteurization batch must be verified via a negative Alkaline Phosphatase test result immediately post-treatment to confirm proper holding temperatures.

## 3. Good Hygienic Practices (GHP) Requirements
- **Facility Layout**: Must feature a direct **forward-flow layout** separating the raw milk receiving dock from pasteurization, processing, and packaging zones to avoid cross-contamination.
- **Location Protection**: The plant must be entirely segregated from residential entry points, clear of flood plains, and structurally protected from external contaminants like stagnant water, open sewers, or toxic fumes.
- **Materials and Equipment**: The use of mild steel or generic plastic containers for storing or transporting milk is strictly prohibited. All surfaces contacting milk must be made of food-grade, corrosion-resistant stainless steel (SS304 or SS316).
- **CIP (Clean-in-Place)**: Automated or strictly documented CIP protocols are mandatory for all storage silos, pasteurizers, homogenizers, and pipe networks.

## 4. Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) & Operational Control
- **Personnel Hygiene**: Every employee working with dairy lines must pass a pre-employment medical examination to guarantee they carry no communicable diseases. Personnel must wear complete headgear enclosing all hair, clean overalls, and waterproof dressings over any minor surface skin wounds.
- **Water Quality**: Water interacting with product zones, ice production, or steam generation must be potable and routinely tested against IS 10500 standards.
- **Air Management**: Processing rooms where open milk or curd handling occurs (e.g., cheese vatting, packaging) require filtered positive-pressure air ventilation systems to block airborne fungal spores and contaminants.
- **Traceability and Testing**: Milk processing plants must maintain active records of procurement origins, batch logs, testing charts, and chemical/microbiological analysis documents for a minimum of 1 year or until product expiration.
