## 1. Introduction to HACCP

The **Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Point (HACCP) system** is a science-based, systematic approach to food safety that identifies, evaluates, and controls hazards that are significant to food safety.

HACCP can be applied throughout the food chain from primary production to final consumption. The HACCP system is based on **7 Principles** and is implemented through **12 Steps** (5 preparatory + 7 principles).

**Benefits of HACCP:**
- Identifies hazards BEFORE they cause illness
- Focuses resources on critical steps in production
- Creates documented evidence of due diligence for regulatory and legal defence
- Enables root cause analysis when deviations occur
- Internationally recognized — accepted by FSSAI, WTO SPS Agreement, and importing country regulators

---

## 2. Prerequisite Programs (PRPs) — Foundation for HACCP

Before implementing HACCP, FBOs must establish and verify **Prerequisite Programs**. PRPs are not CCPs but are essential for the HACCP system to function.

### Core PRPs Required by Codex
1. **Premises and facilities** — design, construction, drainage, pest exclusion
2. **Equipment design** — food-grade materials, cleanability, maintenance
3. **Supplier control program** — approval, specification, certification
4. **Personal hygiene** — training, medical screening, handwashing
5. **Cleaning and disinfection (C&D)** — documented schedules, validated efficacy
6. **Pest control** — integrated pest management
7. **Waste management** — segregation, frequency, records
8. **Water quality** — potability testing, records
9. **Temperature control** — facility HVAC, cold chain continuity
10. **Allergen management** — line scheduling, changeover cleaning, labelling

**Without effective PRPs, HACCP cannot function correctly.** PRPs reduce the background level of contamination; CCPs control specific identified hazards.

## 3. Twelve Steps to HACCP Implementation

### Step 1: Assemble the HACCP Team
- Multidisciplinary team required: production, QA, engineering, procurement, senior management
- At least one member must have formal HACCP training (RSPH, Highfield, or equivalent)
- Scope of the HACCP plan must be defined: specific product(s), specific process, consumer group

### Step 2: Describe the Product
- Complete product description including: composition, physicochemical parameters (pH, Aw, salt), packaging type, storage conditions, shelf life, distribution method, intended use, consumer group (including vulnerable groups: infants, elderly, immunocompromised)

### Step 3: Identify Intended Use
- Consumer group: general public, food service professional, institutional (hospital, school), infant
- Reasonably foreseeable misuse: incorrect storage, inadequate cooking, feeding to vulnerable person

### Step 4: Construct Process Flow Diagram
- All steps from raw material receipt to distribution
- Include: temperatures, times, equipment, decision points
- Must be verified by on-site walkthrough by HACCP team

### Step 5: On-Site Confirmation of Flow Diagram
- Verify flow diagram is accurate during production hours
- Identify any informal practices that deviate from documented procedures

## 4. The Seven HACCP Principles

### Principle 1: Conduct a Hazard Analysis (Step 6)

**What is a hazard?** A biological, chemical, or physical agent in food with the potential to cause an adverse health effect.

**Hazard categories:**
- **Biological:** Pathogenic bacteria, viruses, parasites, toxin-producing organisms (see below)
- **Chemical:** Natural toxins (mycotoxins, plant toxins), processing-generated (acrylamide, nitrosamines), environmental (pesticides, heavy metals, POPs), food additives exceeding permitted levels, allergens
- **Physical:** Hard foreign bodies capable of causing injury — metal, glass, bone, stone (> 2mm threshold for regulatory concern); soft foreign materials (insect, hair) are quality issues but may be safety concerns

**Biological hazards by risk level:**

| Risk Level | Organisms | Key Products |
|---|---|---|
| Very High | Clostridium botulinum (toxin), E. coli O157:H7, Salmonella Typhi | Low-acid canned foods, raw beef, drinking water |
| High | Salmonella, Listeria monocytogenes, Campylobacter, Hepatitis A, Norovirus | Poultry, dairy, RTE foods, shellfish |
| Moderate | Bacillus cereus, Staphylococcus aureus, Clostridium perfringens, Yersinia | Cooked rice, dairy, meat dishes, re-heated foods |
| Low (environmental) | Cronobacter sakazakii | Infant formula, dried spices |

**Hazard Analysis Process:**
1. List all potential hazards for each process step
2. Assess likelihood (1–5) × severity (1–5) = Risk Priority Number (RPN)
3. Determine if control measures exist and are adequate
4. If RPN ≥ threshold AND no adequate control exists → potential CCP

**Significance threshold:** Codex does not specify a fixed RPN threshold; each FBO must define their own based on risk appetite and regulatory requirements. Common practice: RPN ≥ 9 triggers CCP consideration.

### Principle 2: Determine the Critical Control Points (CCPs) — Step 7

**Codex CCP Decision Tree (4 Questions):**

**Q1:** Do control measures exist for this hazard at this step or subsequent steps?
- YES → Go to Q2
- NO → Is control at this step necessary for safety? YES → Modify step/product or redesign process. NO → Not a CCP; this is acceptable.

**Q2:** Is this step specifically designed to eliminate or reduce the likely occurrence of the hazard to an acceptable level?
- YES → **CCP** ✓
- NO → Go to Q3

**Q3:** Could contamination occur at this step in excess of acceptable levels, or could these increase to unacceptable levels?
- YES → Go to Q4
- NO → Not a CCP; control as PRP

**Q4:** Will a subsequent step eliminate the identified hazard or reduce it to acceptable levels?
- YES → Not a CCP (control the hazard at that subsequent step)
- NO → **CCP** ✓

**Important guidance:**
- Not every step is a CCP; too many CCPs suggest inadequate PRP
- CCPs must be distinguishable from PRPs: CCPs have **defined, measurable critical limits** and **mandatory corrective actions** when breached

### Principle 3: Establish Critical Limits — Step 8

**Definition:** Critical limit = the criterion that separates acceptable from unacceptable at a CCP. It must be measurable.

**Scientifically-based critical limits examples:**

| CCP | Biological Hazard | Critical Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Pasteurization (HTST) | Coxiella burnetii, Salmonella, Listeria | ≥ 72°C for ≥ 15 seconds |
| Pasteurization (LTLT) | Same | ≥ 63°C for ≥ 30 minutes |
| Retorting (canning) | Clostridium botulinum | F0 ≥ 3 (121°C equivalent) |
| Cooking poultry | Salmonella, Campylobacter | ≥ 74°C internal |
| Fermentation | Pathogen control via pH | pH ≤ 4.6 throughout |
| Cold chain storage | Growth inhibition | ≤ 5°C (chilled); ≤ -18°C (frozen) |
| Water activity reduction | Growth/toxin production inhibition | Aw ≤ 0.85 (S. aureus toxin); Aw ≤ 0.92 (general) |
| Acidification (canning) | C. botulinum toxin production | pH ≤ 4.6 |

**Critical limits must be:**
- Based on scientific evidence (regulatory standards, validated research, industry guidance)
- Measurable in real-time during production
- Documented with source citations in the HACCP plan

**Operational Limits:** Often set more restrictively than critical limits to provide a safety buffer. Example: Critical limit 72°C → Operational limit 73°C (alarm triggers before CCP breach).

### Principle 4: Establish a Monitoring System — Step 9

**Monitoring purpose:** Detect loss of control at a CCP before unsafe product is produced.

**Monitoring characteristics:**
- **What:** The measurable parameter (temperature, pH, time, chemical concentration)
- **How:** Method of measurement (thermometer, pH meter, visual inspection, chemical test strip)
- **When:** Frequency (continuous, every batch, hourly, per consignment)
- **Who:** Designated responsible person

**Continuous monitoring:** Preferred for CCPs where deviations can be rapid and undetected (e.g., temperature during pasteurization, retort pressure, metal detector performance)

**Monitoring equipment:**
- Must be calibrated at defined frequency
- Calibration records retained for minimum period (2 years or shelf life of product + 1 year, whichever is longer)
- Critical monitoring instruments (pasteurizer recorders): calibrated against NIST/national standards

### Principle 5: Establish Corrective Actions — Step 10

**Purpose:** Define what action to take when monitoring shows a CCP is out of control.

**Two components of corrective action:**
1. **Disposition of affected product:** Hold and assess; may require reprocessing, downgrading, or destruction
2. **Re-establishment of control:** Identify cause of deviation; correct; verify CCP is back in control

**Required elements of corrective action records:**
- Description of deviation (what happened, when, at which CCP)
- Cause of deviation (equipment failure, operator error, raw material issue, power failure)
- Product disposition decision (with basis for decision)
- Preventive action taken to avoid recurrence
- Signatures of responsible persons

**Graduated response examples:**

| Deviation Type | Immediate Action | Corrective Action |
|---|---|---|
| Pasteurizer temperature 1°C below limit for < 5 min | Hold affected product | Adjust flow diversion valve; reprocess product through pasteurizer; investigate cause |
| pH above critical limit in acidified product | Hold and quarantine affected batch | Test pH across batch; reject if borderline; identify cause; correct formulation |
| Metal detector fails check | Hold all product since last successful check | Reinspect product with validated system; identify and remove source of metal |

### Principle 6: Establish Verification Procedures — Step 11

**Purpose:** Confirm the HACCP system is working effectively.

**Verification activities:**
1. **Validation of critical limits** — scientific evidence that the CCP at the stated critical limit actually controls the hazard
2. **Calibration records review** — all monitoring equipment
3. **Direct observation of monitoring** — spot-check at CCP by QA supervisor
4. **Product testing** — microbiological testing of finished product (NOTE: this is verification, not monitoring; testing cannot replace real-time monitoring)
5. **HACCP plan review** — annual review or after any product, process, or personnel change
6. **Internal audits** — at minimum annually; external second-party audits recommended

**Microbiological testing as verification:**
- End-product testing: Confirms that the HACCP system as a whole is effective
- Results used to validate CCPs, not to release product
- Statistically valid sampling plans required (ICMSF sampling plans)

### Principle 7: Establish Documentation and Record Keeping — Step 12

**Mandatory HACCP records:**

| Record Type | Examples | Minimum Retention |
|---|---|---|
| HACCP Plan itself | Hazard analysis worksheets, CCP decision tree records, critical limits with citations | Active + 2 years |
| Monitoring records | Temperature charts, pH logs, metal detector checks | Shelf life + 1 year (minimum 2 years) |
| Corrective action records | Deviation reports, product disposition, preventive actions | 2 years |
| Verification records | Calibration certificates, audit reports, end-product test results | 2 years |
| Prerequisite program records | Cleaning records, pest control logs, supplier certificates | 2 years |

**Record format:**
- Paper records: controlled document format; immediate entry (not retrospective); corrections via single strikethrough + initials
- Electronic records: audit trail; access control; backup protocol

## 5. HACCP Plan Validation

**Validation vs. Verification:**
- **Validation** = evidence that the control measure is effective for the intended purpose (done BEFORE or at time of implementation)
- **Verification** = activities performed AFTER implementation to confirm the system works

**Validation methods:**
1. **Literature review:** Published scientific evidence that e.g. 72°C/15s achieves 5-log reduction of L. monocytogenes
2. **Mathematical modelling:** Predictive microbiology software (ComBase, MicroHibro) to model pathogen growth/inactivation
3. **Challenge testing:** Inoculate product with target pathogen; run through process; confirm ≥ target log reduction
4. **Historical data:** In-process and end-product testing records over time

**When to revalidate:** Any change in: raw materials, formulation, process equipment, packaging, distribution temperature, consumer group, regulatory standards.

## 6. HACCP for Food Service and Catering

Special considerations for non-industrial HACCP (restaurants, institutions):

**Key CCPs in food service:**
| CCP | Hazard | Critical Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Cooking | Salmonella, Campylobacter | ≥ 74°C internal (poultry, ground meat); ≥ 63°C (intact cuts) |
| Hot holding | C. perfringens, B. cereus | ≥ 60°C continuously |
| Cooling of cooked food | C. perfringens, S. aureus | From ≥ 60°C to ≤ 21°C within 2 hours; from 21°C to ≤ 5°C within 4 hours total |
| Cold holding (display) | Listeria, Salmonella | ≤ 5°C at all times |
| Reheating | Pathogen elimination | ≥ 74°C throughout for reheated food; once only |
| Personal hygiene | Norovirus, S. aureus | No bare-hand contact with RTE food; handwash after toilet use |

**The "danger zone":** 5°C to 60°C — temperature range in which food-borne pathogens can grow. The fundamental food service HACCP principle is to minimize time food spends in the danger zone.
