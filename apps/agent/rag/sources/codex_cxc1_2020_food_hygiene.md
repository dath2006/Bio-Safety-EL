# Codex Alimentarius CXC 1-1969 Rev. 2020 — General Principles of Food Hygiene

## The 7 Principles of the HACCP System
The HACCP system consists of the following seven principles:
1. **Conduct a hazard analysis**: Identify significant hazards, evaluate likelihood and severity, and determine control measures.
2. **Determine the Critical Control Points (CCPs)**: Identify the steps in the process where control is essential to prevent, eliminate, or reduce a hazard to an acceptable level.
3. **Establish validated critical limits**: Establish the maximum and/or minimum value to which a biological, chemical or physical parameter must be controlled at a CCP.
4. **Establish a system to monitor control of the CCP**: Establish planned observations or measurements of critical limits to ensure the CCP remains under control.
5. **Establish corrective actions**: Establish actions to be taken when monitoring indicates that a particular CCP is not under control (deviation from critical limits).
6. **Establish procedures for verification**: Establish tests, procedures, and audits to confirm that the HACCP system is working effectively.
7. **Establish documentation and record keeping**: Maintain all procedures and records appropriate to these principles and their application.

## The 4 Codex CCP Decision Tree Questions
The Critical Control Point (CCP) Decision Tree (Annex IV of CXC 1-1969 Rev. 2020) is applied to each process step for hazards determined as significant during hazard analysis. It helps differentiate whether a hazard is managed by Good Hygiene Practices (GHPs) or requires a strict HACCP CCP:

### Q1: Can the significant hazard be controlled to an acceptable level at this step by prerequisite programmes (e.g. Good Hygiene Practices)?
- **Yes**: Stop. The step is **Not a CCP**. Manage the hazard under GHPs.
- **No**: Proceed to **Q2**.

### Q2: Do specific control measures for the identified significant hazard exist at this step?
- **Yes**: Proceed to **Q3**.
- **No**: Modify the step, process, or product to introduce a control measure, and return to Q1.

### Q3: Will a subsequent step prevent or eliminate the identified significant hazard or reduce its likely occurrence to an acceptable level?
- **Yes**: Stop. The step is **Not a CCP**. A subsequent step will control the hazard (and that subsequent step should be evaluated as a potential CCP).
- **No**: Proceed to **Q4**.

### Q4: Does this step specifically prevent or eliminate the identified significant hazard or reduce its likely occurrence to an acceptable level?
- **Yes**: The step is a **Critical Control Point (CCP)**.
- **No**: Stop. The step is **Not a CCP** (the hazard must be evaluated across the rest of the flow to ensure it is controlled elsewhere).

## Good Hygiene Practices (GHPs) as the Foundation
Good Hygiene Practices (GHPs) are the preventive measures and hygiene conditions necessary throughout the food chain to provide safe and suitable food. They are the prerequisite programmes (PRPs) upon which a successful HACCP system is built.

### GHPs include:
- **Design of premises and equipment**: Forward-flow layout, non-porous surfaces, and separation of raw and processed food zones.
- **Control of operation**: Time, temperature, and humidity controls to prevent pathogen growth.
- **Maintenance and sanitation**: Effective cleaning and disinfection programs (including CIP).
- **Personal hygiene**: Health status, cleanliness, protective clothing, and behavior of food handlers.
- **Pest control**: Prevention of access, eradication, and monitoring of pests.
- **Waste management**: Hygienic disposal of waste and by-products.

## Specific Application to Dairy Pasteurization
In a milk pasteurization flow:
- **Hazard**: Survival of vegetative pathogens (Salmonella, Listeria, E. coli) in raw milk.
- **Step**: Pasteurization (heating milk to 72°C for 15 seconds).
- **CCP Decision Tree Logic**:
  - **Q1**: Can it be controlled by GHP alone? **No** (GHP cannot guarantee pathogen destruction in raw milk). Proceed to Q2.
  - **Q2**: Do control measures exist at this step? **Yes** (the pasteurizer heat-treatment). Proceed to Q3.
  - **Q3**: Will a subsequent step control the hazard? **No** (subsequent steps like packaging and cooling do not kill pathogens; they only prevent recontamination). Proceed to Q4.
  - **Q4**: Does this step specifically prevent or eliminate the hazard? **Yes** (pasteurization kills vegetative cells).
  - **Outcome**: Pasteurization is a **Critical Control Point (CCP)**.
